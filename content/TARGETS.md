# Molecular Target Explainers

Deep, plain-English explainers for the key molecules that compounds act on. Keyed by gene symbol. Each is reused wherever that target appears. Written for someone with no science background.

---

## AR
**In one line:** The docking station inside muscle cells that testosterone plugs into to switch on muscle-building.

The **androgen receptor** (AR) is a protein that sits inside your cells, waiting. When testosterone (or a steroid, or a SARM) floats in, it plugs into this receptor like a key into a lock. The locked pair then travels to the cell's DNA and switches on the genes that build muscle, make red blood cells, and strengthen bone.

This one receptor is the single most important target in the whole "build muscle" world. **Every** anabolic steroid, every SARM, and testosterone therapy itself all work by plugging into it. They differ only in how tightly they grip it and which tissues they favour.

The catch: the same receptor exists in your prostate, skin, and scalp — so flooding it (as steroids do) causes side effects far beyond muscle. SARMs were invented to try to hit the receptor mainly in muscle and bone while sparing the rest, though none is perfectly selective.

---

## PRKAA1
**In one line:** The "low-fuel" sensor (AMPK) that tells cells to burn fat and stop hoarding.

PRKAA1 is the main working part of **AMPK** — think of it as the low-fuel warning light in every cell. When your cells run low on energy (from exercise or fasting), this sensor trips and flips the cell into economy mode: burn stored fat, pull in sugar, build more energy factories, and stop wasting fuel making new fat and cholesterol.

This is why the drugs that switch it on — metformin and berberine — help blood sugar and fat loss: they press the same "burn, don't hoard" button that exercise does. It's also the catch for athletes: because these drugs partly copy the exercise signal, they can blunt some training gains.

If your goal is fat loss or healthy metabolism, this is one of the most important switches in your body — and exercise is the free, side-effect-free way to flip it.

---

## MTOR
**In one line:** The master "build" switch — turn it on to grow muscle, off to trigger cellular cleanup.

**mTOR** is a foreman that watches the cell's food supply. When protein and energy are plentiful (after a meal, especially with the amino acid leucine), the foreman shouts "build!" and the cell grows and lays down muscle. When food is scarce, the foreman goes quiet and the crew switches to cleaning and recycling instead.

This is the central tension between fitness and longevity. To build muscle, you *want* to spike mTOR after training — protein does exactly that. But for long-term health, you periodically want it *low* (through fasting, or the drug rapamycin) so cells can clean house — a process linked to longer life in animals.

You can't have it maximally on and off at once. The smart play: spike it around training, and let it fall the rest of the day.

---

## GLP1R
**In one line:** The gut-and-brain "I'm full" receptor that the new weight-loss drugs switch on.

The **GLP-1 receptor** normally responds to a hormone your gut releases after eating. When it's activated, it does several helpful things at once: tells your brain you're full, slows how fast your stomach empties (so you stay full longer), and helps your pancreas manage blood sugar.

The blockbuster weight-loss drugs — semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) — are copies of that gut hormone that switch this receptor on powerfully and for a whole week per dose. The result is a dramatic drop in hunger and "food noise," and 15–22% body-weight loss in trials.

The important catch: these drugs cause muscle loss alongside fat loss, so resistance training and high protein aren't optional extras — they're what keep the weight you lose from being muscle.

---

## ADORA2A
**In one line:** The "you're tired" receptor that caffeine blocks to keep you alert.

Throughout the day, a molecule called adenosine builds up in your brain and lands on the **adenosine A2A receptor**, which signals "you're getting tired." Caffeine works by plugging into that same receptor first — blocking the parking spot so the tiredness signal can't land. You don't get *more* energy; you just stop feeling the fatigue that's there.

This is why caffeine sharpens focus and makes exercise feel easier. It's also why it eventually stops working as well: with daily use, your brain builds *more* of these receptors to compensate (tolerance), so you need more caffeine for the same effect. A 10–14 day break resets it.

And because the tiredness signal is only blocked, not erased, it all comes flooding back when the caffeine wears off — the classic crash.

---

## NOS3
**In one line:** The enzyme that makes nitric oxide, the gas that widens your blood vessels.

**NOS3** (also called eNOS) is the enzyme in your blood-vessel walls that produces **nitric oxide** — a gas signal that tells the vessel to relax and widen. Wider vessels mean more blood flow, more oxygen to your muscles, a better "pump" in the gym, and lower blood pressure.

Supplements like citrulline feed this enzyme more raw material so it can make more nitric oxide. Beetroot takes a shortcut, delivering nitric oxide through a separate route. And erectile-dysfunction drugs work further downstream, stopping the "widen" signal from being broken down.

This is one of the most useful systems for endurance athletes and for heart health — and it's the reason a pre-workout gives you that full, vascular feeling.

---

## HMGCR
**In one line:** The enzyme your liver uses to make cholesterol — and the one statins block.

**HMGCR** is the key enzyme in the assembly line your liver uses to manufacture cholesterol. Statins — the most prescribed heart drugs in the world — work by jamming this enzyme. With less cholesterol being made internally, your liver pulls more of it out of your bloodstream, lowering the "bad" LDL that clogs arteries.

This is one of the best-evidenced interventions in all of medicine for preventing heart attacks. Red yeast rice contains a natural version of a statin that works the same way.

One side note worth knowing: the same assembly line also makes CoQ10, a molecule your muscles' energy factories need. Statins lower it too, which is why some people on statins get muscle aches — and why CoQ10 is often taken alongside.

---

## VDR
**In one line:** The receptor that vitamin D uses to switch on hundreds of genes.

The **vitamin D receptor** (VDR) is how vitamin D does its job. Vitamin D isn't really a vitamin — it's a hormone your skin makes from sunlight. Once activated, it plugs into the VDR, and the pair switches on more than 200 different genes involved in immunity, muscle function, bone strength, and even testosterone production.

Because so much depends on it, being deficient quietly drags down many things at once — mood, immune resilience, muscle quality, and hormones. And deficiency is extremely common in people who work indoors, even in sunny places.

That's why correcting low vitamin D is one of the highest-value, lowest-cost health moves for most people — it's not about megadosing, it's about not being deficient in the first place.

---

## NR3C1
**In one line:** The receptor that the stress hormone cortisol acts through.

**NR3C1** is the **glucocorticoid receptor** — the docking station for cortisol, your main stress hormone. In short bursts, cortisol acting here is useful: it sharpens focus and frees up energy to handle a challenge. The problem is when stress is constant and cortisol stays high for weeks — it starts blocking muscle growth, lowering testosterone, and wrecking sleep.

Adaptogens like ashwagandha and rhodiola work upstream to turn the stress dial down, lowering cortisol. That's why they improve sleep, calm, recovery, and even testosterone in stressed people — they're not sedatives, they're resetting an over-active stress response.

The goal isn't zero cortisol (you need it) — it's a dial that rises when you need it and falls when you don't.

---

## SLC6A3
**In one line:** The "vacuum cleaner" that clears dopamine — blocked by stimulants for focus.

**SLC6A3** is the dopamine transporter — think of it as a tiny vacuum cleaner that sucks the motivation-and-reward chemical dopamine back up after a nerve fires, ending the signal. Focus drugs work by blocking this vacuum, so dopamine lingers longer and the "get-up-and-go" signal stays switched on.

Modafinil blocks it gently (clean, sustained alertness). ADHD stimulants like Adderall block it hard *and* force out more dopamine (powerful focus — and real addiction risk). This is the machinery behind almost every strong focus drug.

The catch with the strong ones: flood dopamine too hard, too often, and the brain compensates by turning down its own supply — that's tolerance and dependence. Gentler is more sustainable.

---

## SLC6A4
**In one line:** The vacuum cleaner for serotonin — blocked by antidepressants to lift mood.

**SLC6A4** is the serotonin transporter — the vacuum that clears serotonin (the calm-contentment chemical) after a signal. Antidepressants called SSRIs block this vacuum, so serotonin stays around longer. Over several weeks, this gradually rewires mood circuits and lifts depression and anxiety.

It's important that the effect takes *weeks*, not hours — the immediate serotonin rise isn't the fix; the slow downstream changes in the brain are. That's why you can't judge an SSRI in a few days.

Some supplements (like saffron, or 5-HTP) nudge serotonin too, but they should never be combined with SSRIs — too much serotonin at once is dangerous (serotonin syndrome).

---

## GHSR
**In one line:** The "hunger and growth-hormone" receptor that ghrelin — and several peptides — switch on.

**GHSR** is the receptor for ghrelin, your hunger hormone. When it's activated, two things happen: you feel hungry, and your pituitary gland releases a pulse of growth hormone. A family of peptides (ipamorelin, MK-677, and others) work by pressing this receptor to raise growth hormone naturally — using your own pituitary rather than injecting synthetic hormone.

The upside: more growth hormone means better recovery, deeper sleep, and fuller muscles. The trade-offs come from the same button: increased appetite and, with the stronger ones, water retention and higher blood sugar.

The appeal of these over injecting growth hormone directly is that they keep the body's natural, pulsing rhythm rather than flooding it constantly.

---

## GHRHR
**In one line:** The pituitary's "release growth hormone" receptor.

**GHRHR** is the receptor on your pituitary gland that responds to your brain's natural "release growth hormone" signal. A family of peptides — sermorelin, CJC-1295, and the FDA-approved tesamorelin — are copies of that signal. They press this receptor to coax your pituitary into releasing more growth hormone in its natural, pulsing pattern.

Paired with a ghrelin-type peptide (which works through a different receptor), the two produce a bigger, more natural growth-hormone pulse than either alone — which is why "CJC-1295 + ipamorelin" is such a common combination.

Tesamorelin is the one member with real FDA approval, uniquely proven to shrink deep belly fat.

---

## MC4R
**In one line:** The brain receptor that controls both appetite and sexual desire.

**MC4R** is a receptor deep in the brain that, surprisingly, governs two different things: how full you feel, and sexual desire. Press it in the appetite centre and hunger drops; press it in the desire circuits and libido rises.

This is why two very different drugs both target it: setmelanotide treats a rare genetic obesity by pressing the "fullness" side, and PT-141 (an approved libido drug) works on the "desire" side — acting in the brain, unlike Viagra-type drugs that work on blood flow.

It's a neat example of how one molecular target can have completely different uses depending on where in the brain it's activated.

---

## GABRA1
**In one line:** Part of the brain's main "brake" — the GABA receptor that calming compounds press.

**GABRA1** is a piece of the GABA-A receptor, the main "brake" in your nervous system. When GABA (or something that mimics it) lands here, the neuron becomes harder to fire — the result is calm, reduced anxiety, and easier sleep.

Gentle compounds like L-theanine and apigenin (from chamomile) nudge this brake softly for relaxed focus and better sleep without knocking you out. Stronger things — alcohol, sleeping pills — press it hard, which sedates but also builds dependence and degrades deep sleep.

Almost everything in the "calm and sleep" category works by pressing this brake to some degree. Gentle and targeted beats hard and blunt.

---

## INSR
**In one line:** The receptor that lets insulin push nutrients into your cells.

The **insulin receptor** is the gateway insulin uses to move sugar and amino acids out of your blood and into your cells. After a meal, insulin docks here and tells cells to take up fuel — storing energy and, in muscle, driving growth.

This makes insulin one of the most powerfully anabolic (muscle-building) signals in the body. It's also why insulin is so dangerous when misused for physique: push too much and blood sugar can crash into a coma. It's among the highest-risk substances misused in the gym.

"Insulin sensitivity" — how well this receptor responds — is central to metabolic health. Exercise, sleep, and drugs like metformin all improve it; constant overeating dulls it.

---

## ESR1
**In one line:** The oestrogen receptor — key to female health, and to managing oestrogen in men on hormones.

**ESR1** is the main **oestrogen receptor**. In women, oestrogen acting here governs the menstrual cycle, bone density, and much of menopausal health — which is why hormone replacement therapy works through it.

It matters for men too. When testosterone is high (from therapy or steroids), some of it converts to oestrogen, which acts here. Drugs called SERMs (like tamoxifen) block this receptor in specific tissues — used to prevent breast-tissue growth in men on steroids, and to restart the body's own testosterone. Other drugs (aromatase inhibitors) cut oestrogen production upstream instead.

The lesson: oestrogen isn't just a "female" hormone — men need some too, and crashing it causes as many problems as having too much.

---

## BDNF
**In one line:** Fertiliser for your brain — it helps brain cells grow, connect, and survive.

**BDNF** (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) is like fertiliser for your brain. It helps neurons grow new connections, survive stress, and form memories. Low BDNF is linked to depression and poor learning; raising it supports mood, memory, and mental resilience.

The best way to raise it is free: exercise, good sleep, and learning. Several compounds (like the peptide semax, and lion's mane mushroom) claim to raise it too. Antidepressants also slowly increase BDNF over weeks, which may be part of why they lift mood.

Think of it as the difference between a garden that's fertilised and one that's neglected — more BDNF, and your brain grows and repairs better.

---

## ADRB2
**In one line:** The "adrenaline" receptor that opens airways and burns fat when switched on.

**ADRB2** (the beta-2 adrenergic receptor) is one of the docks that adrenaline plugs into. Switch it on and airways open (which is why asthma inhalers target it), fat cells release their stored fuel, and the body burns a little hotter.

This is why fat-burners like clenbuterol and ephedrine work — they press this receptor to ramp up fat release and heat production. The same activation, though, speeds the heart and raises blood pressure, which is the downside of strong stimulant fat-burners.

Think of it as the "go faster, burn hotter" switch of your fight-or-flight system.

---

## CHAT
**In one line:** The factory enzyme that builds acetylcholine, your memory-and-focus chemical.

**CHAT** (choline acetyltransferase) is the enzyme that manufactures **acetylcholine** — the brain chemical central to memory, learning, and focus, and the signal your nerves use to fire muscles. It takes raw choline (from eggs or supplements like alpha-GPC) and turns it into the finished product.

Supplements that supply more choline give this factory more raw material to work with, supporting sharper memory and a stronger mind-muscle connection. If the factory runs short of supplies, you get brain fog.

Simply: more raw choline in, more of your "recording and focus" chemical out.

---

## CLEC7A
**In one line:** A sensor on immune cells that "trains" your defences when it detects certain sugars.

**CLEC7A** (Dectin-1) is a sensor sitting on your front-line immune cells. It recognises **beta-glucans** — special sugars found in mushrooms and yeast — and when it does, it primes the immune system to respond faster and stronger to threats. Scientists call this "trained immunity."

This is why beta-glucan and medicinal-mushroom supplements can reduce how often you catch colds — they gently rehearse your immune defences so they're quicker off the mark when a real infection shows up.

Think of it as a fire drill for your immune system.

---

## FFAR2
**In one line:** A gut receptor that responds to the good byproducts your friendly bacteria make.

**FFAR2** is a receptor in your gut that responds to **short-chain fatty acids** — helpful molecules (especially butyrate) that your friendly gut bacteria produce when they ferment fibre. Activating it strengthens the gut lining, calms inflammation, and helps regulate appetite and blood sugar.

This is the science behind prebiotic fibre: you're not adding bacteria, you're *feeding* the good ones you already have so they make more of these beneficial molecules. Feed the garden, and this receptor gets the signals that keep your gut healthy.

---

## GCLC
**In one line:** The enzyme that builds glutathione, your body's master antioxidant.

**GCLC** is the key enzyme that assembles **glutathione** — the most important antioxidant your cells make, and the liver's main tool for neutralising toxins. Glutathione is like your cells' rust-protection: it mops up the damaging molecules that ageing, pollution, and hard training generate.

The catch is that building it needs a specific raw material (the amino acid cysteine) that often runs short. That's why NAC — which supplies exactly that raw material — restocks glutathione so effectively, and why it's the hospital antidote for paracetamol overdose (which floods the liver and drains glutathione).

More raw material in, more master antioxidant out.

---

## GIPR
**In one line:** A second gut "fullness" receptor that the newest weight-loss drugs hit alongside GLP-1.

**GIPR** responds to another gut hormone (called GIP) that helps manage blood sugar and appetite. On its own it's modest, but paired with the GLP-1 receptor it becomes powerful — which is exactly what tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) does: it presses *both* "fullness" receptors at once, producing even greater weight loss than hitting GLP-1 alone.

Think of it as a second dimmer switch for hunger. One switch helps; turning down both together helps a lot more — which is why the dual drugs outperform the single ones.

---

## NAMPT
**In one line:** The enzyme that recycles NAD⁺, the fuel your cellular repair crews run on.

**NAMPT** is the key enzyme in the recycling line that keeps your cells stocked with **NAD⁺** — the molecule that powers energy production and your DNA-repair and anti-ageing crews. NAD⁺ levels fall by about half between ages 20 and 50, partly because this recycling slows down.

NAD⁺-boosting supplements (NMN, NR) feed this recycling line more raw material so it can keep the tank full. It's the machinery behind one of the most exciting areas in longevity science — though the human benefits look real but modest, and matter most after 40.

---

## SHBG
**In one line:** A carrier protein that "handcuffs" testosterone — lowering it frees more up.

**SHBG** (sex-hormone-binding globulin) is a protein that grabs onto testosterone in your blood and holds it, making it unavailable to your muscles. Only the "free" testosterone that isn't handcuffed can actually do its job.

This is why some supplements (like boron and tongkat ali) can help even without raising *total* testosterone — they lower SHBG, so more of the testosterone you already have gets released to work. If most of your testosterone is handcuffed, freeing even a little makes a real difference.

---

## SIRT1
**In one line:** A repair-crew enzyme that switches on longevity and maintenance programs — powered by NAD⁺.

**SIRT1** is one of the **sirtuins** — enzymes that act like cellular maintenance foremen. When switched on, they turn on genes for DNA repair, healthy mitochondria, and stress resistance — programs linked to longer, healthier life in animals. They run on the fuel NAD⁺, which declines with age.

This is why the longevity world cares about both NAD⁺ boosters (which supply the fuel) and compounds like resveratrol (which activate the sirtuins directly). Fasting and exercise switch them on for free. Think of them as the crew that keeps the cell in good repair — as long as they have fuel.

---

## TH
**In one line:** The factory enzyme that builds dopamine, your motivation-and-drive chemical.

**TH** (tyrosine hydroxylase) is the rate-limiting enzyme that manufactures **dopamine** (and downstream, noradrenaline) — the brain chemicals behind motivation, focus, and alertness. "Rate-limiting" just means it's the slowest step, so it sets how much dopamine you can make.

Supplying the raw material (the amino acid tyrosine) helps most when you're depleted — under stress, cold, or sleep loss — by giving this factory more to work with. Some compounds (like bromantane) work by increasing the *amount* of this enzyme, so you build more dopamine over time rather than just releasing what you have.

---

## ADRA2A
**In one line:** A "brake" on fat-burning that yohimbine blocks to unlock stubborn fat.

**ADRA2A** (the alpha-2A adrenergic receptor) is an odd one: it actually *blocks* fat release in stubborn areas (lower belly, hips, thighs). Yohimbine works by jamming this brake — so those stubborn fat cells can finally release their fuel, especially during fasted cardio.

That's why yohimbine is used specifically for "stubborn fat" once you're already lean. The catch: blocking this brake also revs up your fight-or-flight system, which can cause anxiety and a racing heart in sensitive people.

Think of it as releasing the parking brake on the fat that won't budge.

---

## CALCR
**In one line:** The receptor for amylin, a second "I'm full" hormone stacked with GLP-1 drugs.

**CALCR** (the calcitonin/amylin receptor) responds to **amylin**, a hormone that works alongside insulin to slow digestion and signal fullness. It's a *different* fullness pathway from GLP-1 — which is why the newest experimental combos (cagrilintide plus semaglutide) stack them: two separate "I'm full" systems switched on together for greater appetite control.

Think of it as a second, independent hunger dimmer. Turning down two different ones at once beats leaning on just one.

---

## CHRNA4
**In one line:** A nicotine receptor that, when activated, sharpens attention and reaction time.

**CHRNA4** is part of the **nicotinic acetylcholine receptor** — one of the docks nicotine plugs into. Activating it genuinely sharpens attention, working memory, and reaction speed, which is why nicotine (separated from smoking) has real cognitive effects.

The problem is the same receptor sits in the brain's reward circuit, so activating it is strongly addictive. That's the trade-off: real focus benefit, real dependence risk. For most people the addiction isn't worth it.

---

## DRD2
**In one line:** A dopamine receptor used to control the hormone prolactin.

**DRD2** (the dopamine D2 receptor) has many roles, but one practical use stands out: dopamine acting here tells the body to *lower* prolactin, a hormone that, when too high, kills libido and causes other problems. Certain steroids (the "19-nor" family, like nandrolone) raise prolactin, so users take a drug called cabergoline that switches on this receptor to keep prolactin in check.

Simply: press this dopamine button, and prolactin comes down.

---

## GABBR1
**In one line:** A slower "calm" receptor — the one phenibut presses (with a dependence risk).

**GABBR1** is the GABA-B receptor, a slower-acting version of the brain's main "brake." When activated it produces calm and sociability. Phenibut works here — which is why it reduces anxiety and lowers inhibitions.

The serious catch: pressing this receptor builds tolerance fast, and stopping causes a genuinely nasty withdrawal. It's easy to get into trouble with, so it's a "handle with real caution" compound rather than a casual calming aid.

---

## GCGR
**In one line:** The glucagon receptor — the third switch in the most powerful weight-loss drug in trials.

**GCGR** responds to **glucagon**, a hormone that raises energy expenditure and tells the liver to burn fat. On its own that's not a diet tool, but the experimental drug retatrutide adds glucagon-receptor activation on *top* of the two "fullness" receptors (GLP-1 and GIP) — so it cuts hunger *and* turns up calorie-burning at once. That triple action produced the largest weight loss ever recorded for a drug (about 24% of body weight).

Think of it as the "burn more" switch added to the "eat less" switches.

---

## GH1
**In one line:** The gene for growth hormone itself — the body's build-repair-and-lean hormone.

**GH1** is the gene that makes **growth hormone**, which drives tissue repair, fat-burning, and (indirectly) muscle growth. Peptides like the "fragment" AOD-9604 are pieces of this hormone designed to keep just the fat-burning part without the muscle or blood-sugar effects.

Growth hormone is powerful but double-edged: helpful for genuine deficiency, but misused for physique it causes insulin resistance, swelling, and organ growth. More isn't automatically better.

---

## GHR
**In one line:** The receptor growth hormone plugs into to do its work.

**GHR** (the growth hormone receptor) is the dock that growth hormone lands on to trigger its effects — releasing fat, building tissue, and telling the liver to produce IGF-1 (a key muscle-growth signal). Injecting actual growth hormone works directly here, unlike the peptides that instead nudge your own pituitary to release more.

The direct approach is potent but carries the real risks of flooding the system: insulin resistance, swelling, and, because it's a growth signal, a tumour-growth caution.

---

## GLRA1
**In one line:** A "calming brake" in the spinal cord that glycine presses to help you relax and sleep.

**GLRA1** is the glycine receptor — another of the nervous system's "brakes," concentrated in the spinal cord and brainstem. The amino acid **glycine** presses it, which calms the nervous system and, combined with glycine's cooling effect on body temperature, helps you fall asleep faster.

This is part of why a few grams of glycine before bed improves sleep — it's gently pressing a calming brake while lowering your core temperature, both of which signal "time to sleep." Cheap and very safe.

---

## GRIA1
**In one line:** The brain's main "accelerator" receptor, tuned up gently by racetam nootropics.

**GRIA1** is part of the **AMPA receptor**, which glutamate (the brain's main "go" signal) acts through to make neurons fire — the basis of alertness and learning. Racetam nootropics (like piracetam) gently tune up this receptor, which may sharpen memory and clarity.

Think of glutamate as the accelerator pedal; these compounds make the pedal a little more responsive. Pairing them with a choline source is standard, because pushing the accelerator harder also uses up more of the "focus" chemical acetylcholine.

---

## GRIN1
**In one line:** A learning-and-memory receptor that ketamine blocks — with a rapid antidepressant effect.

**GRIN1** is part of the **NMDA receptor**, central to learning and memory. Ketamine blocks it, which paradoxically triggers a surge of brain growth signals (BDNF) and rapidly regrows lost connections — lifting severe depression within *hours* instead of the weeks antidepressants take.

That's why a form of ketamine is now an approved treatment for depression that hasn't responded to other drugs. It's given under supervision because blocking this receptor also causes a dissociative, dream-like state.

---

## HCRT
**In one line:** Your brain's "stay awake" signal — the orexin system.

**HCRT** (orexin) is the brain chemical that keeps you awake and alert; losing the cells that make it causes narcolepsy. Wakefulness drugs like modafinil partly work by boosting this "stay awake" system.

Its opposite is just as useful: the newest sleep drugs (orexin *blockers*) switch this system off to let you sleep — a cleaner approach than older sedatives, because it removes the "awake" signal rather than force-sedating the whole brain.

---

## HCRTR2
**In one line:** The "stay awake" receptor — blocking it is how modern sleep drugs work.

**HCRTR2** is one of the receptors the wake-promoting orexin signal acts on. The newest prescription sleep drugs (suvorexant, lemborexant) *block* it — quietly switching off the brain's "stay awake" command so natural sleep can take over.

This is gentler than older sleeping pills that hammer the brain's brake and cause grogginess and dependence. Here you're just letting the wakefulness signal fade, so sleep architecture stays more natural.

---

## HTR2A
**In one line:** The receptor psychedelics act on — linked to a burst of brain plasticity.

**HTR2A** (the 5-HT2A serotonin receptor) is the main dock for classic psychedelics like psilocybin. Activating it sets off a burst of neuroplasticity — the brain forming new connections — which, paired with therapy, is showing real promise for depression that hasn't responded to other treatments.

Full doses under supervision are where the strong evidence is; "microdosing" (tiny sub-perceptual doses) mostly performs no better than placebo in controlled trials, despite the hype.

---

## IGF1
**In one line:** The powerful muscle-and-tissue growth signal that growth hormone works through.

**IGF-1** is a growth factor — one of the body's strongest signals to build muscle and repair tissue. Much of growth hormone's effect actually happens through IGF-1, which the liver releases in response. Some athletes inject a long-acting version directly for its potent muscle-building effect.

The double edge is sharp: the very same "grow" signal that builds muscle can also feed tumours and drop blood sugar dangerously — which is why injected IGF-1 is high-risk and why growth-factor drugs carry cancer cautions.

---

## IGF1R
**In one line:** The receptor that the muscle-growth signal IGF-1 plugs into.

**IGF1R** is the dock for **IGF-1**, the body's powerful "build and repair tissue" signal. When IGF-1 lands here, it drives muscle growth and cell survival — and feeds into the main mTOR "build" switch.

It's a genuinely anabolic target, which is exactly why it's risky when pushed artificially: the same "grow and survive" command that builds muscle can also help tumour cells grow. Powerful, and not to be poked casually.

---

## MC1R
**In one line:** The skin's pigment receptor — activating it produces a tan.

**MC1R** is the receptor on skin cells that controls pigment. Activate it and your skin produces more melanin — a tan. The compound melanotan works by pressing this receptor to tan skin with less sun exposure.

The concern is real: it also darkens existing moles, and its long-term safety (including melanoma risk) is unknown. So while it does produce a tan, it sits firmly in the "unresolved safety questions" category.

---

## ABL1
**In one line:** A "grow" switch inside certain cells that a leukaemia drug shuts off — also used to kill ageing "zombie" cells.

**ABL1** is a signalling enzyme that, when stuck on, drives cells to grow — a known cause of one type of leukaemia. The cancer drug dasatinib blocks it. In longevity research, dasatinib is repurposed (paired with quercetin) as a **senolytic** — it helps clear "zombie" cells that refuse to die and leak inflammation. Because it's a real chemotherapy drug, this belongs strictly under medical supervision, not DIY.

---

## ALOX5
**In one line:** An enzyme that makes a specific kind of inflammation — one that ordinary painkillers miss.

**ALOX5** (5-lipoxygenase) builds **leukotrienes**, inflammatory messengers that drive joint pain through a route that common anti-inflammatories (like ibuprofen) don't touch. Boswellia (frankincense extract) blocks this enzyme, which is why it helps joint pain from a different angle and is often stacked with curcumin for broader coverage.

Think of inflammation as coming from several taps — this one shuts a tap the usual drugs leave running.

---

## BCL2L1
**In one line:** A "survival" protein that zombie cells hide behind — senolytics strip it away.

**BCL2L1** is one of the proteins that keep cells alive by blocking self-destruction. Worn-out "zombie" (senescent) cells over-rely on it to avoid dying — so they linger and leak inflammation. Senolytic compounds like fisetin and quercetin work partly by blocking this survival shield, so the zombie cells finally self-destruct and get cleared. That's why senolytics are pulsed a couple of days at a time rather than taken daily.

---

## BHMT
**In one line:** A recycling enzyme that betaine feeds to support methylation and creatine production.

**BHMT** is an enzyme that recycles homocysteine (a molecule that's harmful when it builds up) back into useful methionine, using **betaine** as the helper. This supports "methylation" — a background chemistry your body uses for countless jobs — and frees up materials for making creatine. It's part of why betaine is a modest strength-and-power supplement, and why it supports healthy homocysteine levels.

---

## CAMP
**In one line:** The gene for a natural antibiotic your own body makes.

**CAMP** makes **LL-37**, a natural antimicrobial your body produces to punch holes in bacteria and help wounds heal. It's being explored as a supplement peptide for infection and healing, but the evidence is very early. Interesting biology, minimal human proof so far. (Note: don't confuse the gene name "CAMP" with the messenger molecule "cAMP" from the doorbell pathway — different things.)

---

## Cardiolipin
**In one line:** The special "insulation" inside your cellular power plants — repairing it restores clean energy.

**Cardiolipin** is a unique fat that lines the inner wall of your mitochondria (the cell's power plants) and keeps their energy-making machinery wired correctly. With age and disease it gets damaged, so the plants leak and make energy less cleanly. The peptide elamipretide (SS-31) works by stabilising cardiolipin — think of it as repairing the insulation on the wiring so the power flows properly again. It's the furthest-along of the mitochondrial-repair drugs.

---

## CARNS1
**In one line:** The enzyme that builds carnosine, your muscles' acid-sponge for hard efforts.

**CARNS1** (carnosine synthase) builds **carnosine**, which acts as a sponge for the acid that floods your muscles during hard, sustained effort — the "burn" that forces you to stop. More carnosine means you can push through that burn longer.

Beta-alanine is the raw material this enzyme needs, which is why taking it daily for a few weeks raises muscle carnosine and improves performance in efforts lasting one to four minutes — exactly the range that makes you want to quit.

---

## CASR
**In one line:** The body's calcium thermostat — it keeps blood calcium steady.

**CASR** (the calcium-sensing receptor) is your body's calcium thermostat: it senses how much calcium is in your blood and adjusts things to keep it in a tight, safe range. This matters because calcium runs your nerves, muscles, and heartbeat — too much or too little is dangerous.

For bone health, the practical point is that calcium alone isn't enough: you need vitamin D to absorb it and vitamin K2 to steer it into bone rather than arteries. The thermostat handles the rest.

---

## COL1A1
**In one line:** The gene for type-1 collagen — the rope-like protein in your tendons, skin, and bone.

**COL1A1** makes **type-1 collagen**, the main structural protein in tendons, ligaments, skin, and bone — essentially the body's scaffolding and rope. Collagen supplements supply the specific building blocks (with vitamin C as a required helper) to support this scaffolding, especially when you take them about an hour before loading the tissue in training.

That timing matters: it primes your tendons to rebuild stronger right when you stress them. Very relevant if you run or train hard and want your joints to keep up.

---

## COMT
**In one line:** An enzyme that clears your "alertness" chemicals — slowing it makes their effect last longer.

**COMT** breaks down noradrenaline and dopamine, ending their "alert and focused" signal. Green tea's EGCG partly blocks this enzyme, so those signals linger a bit longer — which is why green tea extract has a mild fat-burning and focus effect, especially alongside caffeine.

Interestingly, people naturally vary in how fast their COMT works, which is part of why some handle caffeine and stress very differently from others.

---

## COQ2
**In one line:** An enzyme in the assembly line that builds CoQ10, a spark plug for your cellular power plants.

**COQ2** helps build **CoQ10**, an essential part of the energy-making chain inside your mitochondria and a fat-based antioxidant. Statins accidentally lower CoQ10 (they share an assembly line with cholesterol), which is one reason some statin users get muscle aches — and why CoQ10 is often taken alongside statins.

Think of CoQ10 as a spark plug in your cellular engines: run low and the engines sputter, especially in the hard-working heart and muscles.

---

## CYP19A1
**In one line:** The enzyme that turns testosterone into oestrogen — blocked to control oestrogen on hormone cycles.

**CYP19A1** (aromatase) is the enzyme that converts some of your testosterone into oestrogen. Men on testosterone therapy or steroids can see oestrogen climb too high (causing bloating and breast tissue), so "aromatase inhibitor" drugs (like anastrozole) block this enzyme to keep it in check.

The important nuance: the goal is *balance*, not zero. Crashing oestrogen causes just as many problems — bad joints, low libido, poor mood — as having too much. It's a dial to tune, not a switch to slam off.

---

## DIO2
**In one line:** The enzyme that switches thyroid hormone into its active form — your metabolism's activator.

**DIO2** converts the storage form of thyroid hormone (T4) into the active form (T3) that actually revs up your metabolism. It needs the mineral **selenium** to work. Your thyroid is the body's metabolic thermostat, built from iodine and switched on by selenium — which is why both minerals matter for energy and metabolism.

If this conversion runs poorly, you can feel sluggish even with "normal" thyroid levels, because the active hormone isn't being made.

---

## EP300
**In one line:** A brake on cellular "self-cleaning" that spermidine releases to slow ageing.

**EP300** normally holds back **autophagy** — your cells' recycling-and-cleanup program. The longevity compound spermidine works partly by blocking EP300, which lets autophagy switch on so cells clear out damaged parts, much as fasting does. Populations who eat more spermidine (from wheat germ and aged cheese) tend to live longer.

Think of it as taking your foot off the brake so the cell's cleanup crew can get to work.

---

## EPOR
**In one line:** The receptor that tells your bone marrow to make more oxygen-carrying red blood cells.

**EPOR** responds to **EPO** (erythropoietin), the signal that drives production of red blood cells — which carry oxygen. Boosting it raises your blood's oxygen-carrying capacity, a huge endurance advantage. That's why synthetic EPO defined cycling's doping era.

The danger is the flip side: more red cells thicken the blood, sharply raising the risk of clots and stroke. Powerful and genuinely dangerous — and banned in sport.

---

## ESR2
**In one line:** A second oestrogen receptor that plant "ecdysteroids" may work through — non-hormonally.

**ESR2** (oestrogen receptor beta) is a cousin of the main oestrogen receptor, but activating it doesn't cause typical hormonal (feminising) effects. It's the proposed route for ecdysterone — the plant compound behind "natural anabolic" hype — which may nudge muscle protein synthesis through this receptor rather than through testosterone.

The evidence is thin (one suggestive human study for ecdysterone; almost none for turkesterone), so temper expectations. It's not working like a steroid.

---

## FFAR4/GPR120
**In one line:** A receptor that senses omega-3 fats and uses them to calm inflammation.

**FFAR4** (also called GPR120) is a sensor that detects **omega-3** fatty acids and, when they dock, dials down inflammation and improves how your body handles sugar and fat. It's one of the ways fish oil produces its wide-ranging benefits for the heart, joints, and metabolism.

Simply: it's a receptor that "reads" the good fats you eat and turns that into an anti-inflammatory signal throughout the body.

---

## Follistatin
**In one line:** A natural brake-release for muscle growth — it neutralises the "stop growing" signal myostatin.

**Follistatin** binds and neutralises **myostatin**, the protein your body uses to *cap* how much muscle you build. Less myostatin means the cap comes off and muscles can grow larger — the same reason certain super-muscular cattle and dogs exist. Experimental "myostatin inhibitor" compounds aim to copy this.

Exciting in theory, but human safety is unknown and early trials of related drugs were stopped for side effects. Promising mechanism, unproven and risky in people.

---

## GSK3B
**In one line:** An enzyme that low-dose lithium calms — possibly protecting the ageing brain.

**GSK3B** is an enzyme involved in many cellular processes; calming it appears to protect neurons and switch on cellular cleanup. Tiny doses of lithium (far below psychiatric doses) inhibit it, which may be why regions with more natural lithium in their water show slightly lower death and suicide rates. Very early evidence — intriguing, not established.

---

## KCNJ11
**In one line:** A tiny gate on hair-follicle blood vessels that minoxidil opens to grow hair.

**KCNJ11** is part of a potassium channel — a gate on cells. Minoxidil opens these gates on the blood vessels feeding hair follicles, widening them and keeping hairs in their growth phase longer. That's why minoxidil, paired with finasteride, is a core evidence-based hair-loss treatment.

Think of it as improving the "plumbing and lease" for each follicle: better blood supply, and a longer stay in the growing phase.

---

## KDR/VEGFR2
**In one line:** The "grow new blood vessels" receptor that the healing peptide BPC-157 switches on.

**KDR** (also called VEGFR2) is the main receptor for growing new blood vessels. That matters for healing, because injured tissue can only repair if it gets a fresh blood supply. The peptide BPC-157 appears to switch this receptor on, driving new vessel growth to tendons, gut, and muscle — which is why it shows such striking healing in animal studies.

The honest catch: almost all of that evidence is in rats. In humans it's largely unproven, and growing new blood vessels is also something tumours do — a theoretical caution.

---

## LHCGR
**In one line:** The "make testosterone" receptor on the testicles that HCG switches on.

**LHCGR** is the receptor on testicular cells that responds to the brain's "make testosterone" signal (LH). The drug **HCG** mimics that signal and presses this receptor directly — which keeps the testicles working (and full-sized) even when someone is on testosterone therapy, which would otherwise shut them down.

That's why HCG is used alongside testosterone to preserve fertility and testicle size: it keeps the local factory running when the brain's own order has been switched off.

---

## LTF
**In one line:** An iron-grabbing protein in milk that starves bacteria and supports immunity.

**LTF** makes **lactoferrin**, a protein found in milk and colostrum that grabs iron (which bacteria need to grow) and directly disrupts microbes — so it starves and fights infections while supporting the gut and immune system. It's why lactoferrin supplements are used for immune and gut support.

Think of it as an immune helper that works partly by locking away the iron that invading bacteria are hungry for.

---

## MAOA
**In one line:** The enzyme that clears mood chemicals — blocking it raises them (and demands caution).

**MAOA** (monoamine oxidase A) breaks down serotonin, dopamine, and noradrenaline — your mood and drive chemicals. Blocking it raises all three. Methylene blue and a few compounds do this mildly.

The serious catch: because blocking this enzyme raises serotonin, combining an MAO-blocker with other serotonin-raising things (SSRIs, 5-HTP) can cause dangerous serotonin overload. So compounds that block it must be handled carefully around other mood drugs.

---

## MET
**In one line:** A "build new connections" receptor in the brain that the peptide dihexa targets.

**MET** (the c-Met receptor) responds to a growth factor that, in the brain, drives the formation of new synapses — the connections behind learning and memory. The experimental peptide dihexa is reported to supercharge this in animals, making it a striking (but entirely unproven-in-humans) nootropic.

As with any "growth" pathway, there's a built-in caution: signals that grow new connections can, in principle, also help tumours grow. No human safety data exists.

---

## MGAM
**In one line:** A gut enzyme that digests carbs — slowing it blunts blood-sugar spikes.

**MGAM** is an intestinal enzyme that breaks starches down into sugar for absorption. The drug acarbose partially blocks it, so carbs are digested more slowly and blood sugar rises in a gentle trickle instead of a spike — a bit like a mild low-carb effect in a pill. It's also one of the more reproducible life-extenders in animal studies.

The trade-off: undigested carbs travel further down the gut and ferment, which causes gas and bloating.

---

## MTHFR
**In one line:** The enzyme that activates folate — some people have a slower version and do better with the pre-activated form.

**MTHFR** converts folate (vitamin B9) into its active form, which your body needs for "methylation" — background chemistry that runs DNA, mood, and detox. A common gene variant makes this enzyme slower, so those people may do better taking the already-active form (methylfolate) rather than plain folic acid.

It's why quality B-vitamin supplements use the "methylated" active forms — they skip the step a sluggish enzyme struggles with.

---

## MTNR1A
**In one line:** The receptor for melatonin — your body's "it's night time" signal.

**MTNR1A** is a receptor for **melatonin**, the hormone your brain releases in darkness to say "it's night." It doesn't knock you out like a sedative — it re-times your internal body clock. That's why melatonin works best for jet lag and for people whose sleep runs late, rather than as a sledgehammer.

The common mistake is dosing far too high: a tiny amount (0.5 mg) shifts the clock as well as 10 mg, with less next-morning grogginess.

---

## NFE2L2/NRF2
**In one line:** The master switch for your body's own antioxidant and detox defences.

**NRF2** is a master switch that, when flipped on, turns on around 200 of your body's own antioxidant and detox genes — your built-in defence system against damage. Sulforaphane, from broccoli sprouts, is the best-known natural activator.

The clever part: instead of *supplying* antioxidants from outside, this switches on your body's *own* defence factory, which is far more powerful and long-lasting. Broccoli sprouts are the richest source.

---

## NGF
**In one line:** "Nerve fertiliser" — it helps brain and nerve cells grow and survive.

**NGF** (nerve growth factor) is fertiliser specifically for nerve and brain cells — it supports their growth, maintenance, and survival. Lion's mane mushroom is popular because its compounds nudge the brain to make more NGF, which over months may support memory and reduce brain fog.

Like its cousin BDNF, this is a "grow and repair" signal for the brain — slow-acting, best over the long term, and supported by learning and healthy living too.

---

## NR1D1
**In one line:** A body-clock gene that, when nudged, tells muscles to build more energy factories.

**NR1D1** (REV-ERB alpha) is part of your internal 24-hour clock, and it also controls how many mitochondria (energy factories) your muscles build. The research compound SR9009 activates it, which in mice increased endurance and metabolic rate — an "exercise in a bottle" idea.

The reality check: it's barely absorbed when taken by mouth, so the impressive mouse results probably don't carry over to humans taking it as a pill.

---

## OPRM1
**In one line:** The main opioid receptor — blocking it curbs cravings and reward-eating.

**OPRM1** (the mu-opioid receptor) handles reward and pleasure — including from food. The weight drug Contrave pairs a blocker of this receptor with an appetite-suppressant, which together dampen cravings and reward-driven eating. So it targets *why* people overeat (reward) rather than gut fullness — useful for emotional or craving-led eating.

Blocking the reward receptor takes some of the "pull" out of tempting food.

---

## P4HA1
**In one line:** The enzyme that builds collagen — and it can't work without vitamin C.

**P4HA1** is a key enzyme in making **collagen**, the scaffolding of your skin, tendons, and bone. Crucially, it needs **vitamin C** to function — which is the whole reason severe vitamin-C deficiency (scurvy) makes the body fall apart.

The practical takeaway: taking collagen with vitamin C isn't optional if you want to actually build connective tissue, because this enzyme literally can't finish the job without the vitamin.

---

## PCSK9
**In one line:** A protein that destroys your "bad-cholesterol vacuum cleaners" — blocking it slashes LDL.

Your liver has receptors that act like vacuum cleaners, pulling bad (LDL) cholesterol out of your blood. **PCSK9** is a protein that destroys those vacuums. Newer heart drugs block PCSK9 so the vacuums survive longer and clear far more LDL — dropping it dramatically. One version (inclisiran) silences PCSK9 with an injection needed only twice a year.

It's one of the most powerful cholesterol-lowering tools ever made, used when statins aren't enough.

---

## PDE5A
**In one line:** The enzyme that ends the "widen blood vessels" signal — Viagra-type drugs block it.

**PDE5A** breaks down the messenger that keeps blood vessels relaxed and open. Erectile-dysfunction drugs (sildenafil, tadalafil) block this enzyme, so the "stay widened" signal lasts longer and blood flow is maintained where it's needed. Daily low-dose tadalafil is also studied for broader blood-vessel and prostate benefits.

Important safety point: never combine these with nitrates or high-dose beetroot — together they can drop blood pressure dangerously.

---

## PPARA
**In one line:** A master switch that tells the body to burn fat for fuel.

**PPARA** is a switch that, when activated, turns on the genes for burning fat as fuel — especially in the liver and during fasting. Omega-3 fats activate it, which is part of how fish oil lowers blood triglycerides (a type of blood fat).

Think of it as a "burn fat for energy" program that certain foods and fasting switch on.

---

## PPARD
**In one line:** An "endurance" switch that tells muscles to burn fat and last longer.

**PPARD** (PPAR-delta) is a switch that shifts muscle toward burning fat for fuel and boosts endurance — essentially mimicking some effects of exercise. The compound cardarine (GW-501516) activates it, and in animals it dramatically increased running endurance.

The serious catch, and the reason it was abandoned: in animal studies it caused cancer in multiple organs at multiple doses. Impressive endurance effect, unacceptable safety signal.

---

## RARA
**In one line:** The vitamin-A receptor that retinoids use to renew skin and build collagen.

**RARA** (a retinoic acid receptor) is how vitamin-A-based skincare (retinoids like tretinoin) works. Activating it speeds up skin-cell renewal and switches on collagen production — which is why retinoids are the single most proven ingredient for wrinkles and acne.

Everything else in skincare is secondary to this. The trade-off is irritation early on, so you introduce it slowly.

---

## SLC1A5
**In one line:** A doorway that pulls glutamine into cells — fuel for your gut lining.

**SLC1A5** is a transporter that moves the amino acid **glutamine** into cells. Glutamine is the main fuel for the cells lining your gut, which is why glutamine supplements are used to support a damaged or "leaky" gut, especially after heavy training or illness. For a healthy person eating enough protein, the benefit is smaller.

---

## SLC23A1
**In one line:** The doorway that pulls vitamin C into your cells.

**SLC23A1** is a transporter that moves **vitamin C** into cells, where it's needed to build collagen, make certain brain chemicals, and act as an antioxidant. Your body can't make vitamin C, so you depend entirely on getting it from food or supplements and moving it inside via this doorway.

It's a reminder that vitamin C isn't just for colds — it's a required helper for building connective tissue and more.

---

## SLC39A
**In one line:** The doorways that carry zinc into your cells.

**SLC39A** refers to the family of transporters (called ZIP) that move **zinc** into your cells. Zinc is a helper in over a hundred reactions, including making testosterone and running your immune system. Athletes lose zinc in sweat, so deficiency is common — and it directly lowers testosterone.

Fixing a zinc shortage reliably helps hormones and immunity; taking extra when you're already fine does little.

---

## SLC5A2
**In one line:** The kidney's sugar-reabsorbing gate — blocking it makes you pee out excess sugar.

**SLC5A2** (SGLT2) is a gate in your kidneys that normally reclaims sugar back into your blood. The newer diabetes drugs (empagliflozin, canagliflozin) block it, so excess sugar leaves in your urine instead. That creates a mild calorie loss and metabolic benefit, with strong evidence for protecting the heart and kidneys — and an emerging longevity signal.

Simply: it lets you pee out some of the sugar you'd otherwise store.

---

## SLC6A2
**In one line:** The vacuum cleaner for noradrenaline — blocked to raise alertness and curb appetite.

**SLC6A2** is the transporter that clears **noradrenaline**, an alertness-and-arousal chemical. Blocking it keeps noradrenaline around longer — raising focus and suppressing appetite. Some ADHD medicines and the older weight drug phentermine work partly this way.

It's the "stay alert" counterpart to the dopamine vacuum, and a reason certain focus and appetite drugs overlap.

---

## SLC6A6
**In one line:** The doorway that carries taurine into your heart and muscle cells.

**SLC6A6** moves the amino acid **taurine** into cells, where it stabilises the heart and muscles, helps manage calcium, and acts as an antioxidant. Taurine levels fall with age, and in a major animal study, topping it up extended healthy lifespan.

It's cheap, safe, and increasingly interesting for both endurance and longevity — this doorway is simply how it gets where it's needed.

---

## SLC6A8
**In one line:** The doorway that pulls creatine into your muscles.

**SLC6A8** is the transporter that moves **creatine** into muscle cells, where it recharges your cells' rapid-energy "battery" for short, explosive effort. Supplementing creatine keeps this battery topped up beyond what food alone provides — giving you an extra rep or two and faster recovery between hard sets.

It's the delivery door for the most proven, cheapest performance supplement there is.

---

## SRD5A2
**In one line:** The enzyme that turns testosterone into a stronger form (DHT) — blocked to save hair.

**SRD5A2** converts testosterone into **DHT**, a more potent version that drives male-pattern hair loss and prostate growth. Hair-loss drugs (finasteride, dutasteride) block this enzyme to protect the scalp.

The trade-off worth knowing: for a minority of men, lowering DHT dents libido or mood, and occasionally these effects persist. Very effective for hair — weigh that risk honestly.

---

## TERT
**In one line:** The enzyme that rebuilds the protective caps on your DNA (telomeres).

**TERT** is the working part of **telomerase**, the enzyme that rebuilds "telomeres" — the protective caps on the ends of your chromosomes that shorten each time a cell divides and with age. The peptide epithalon is claimed to switch it on to lengthen these caps.

Two cautions: the evidence is largely old and hard to verify, and switching telomerase on is also something cancer cells do — so it's an intriguing but unproven longevity idea.

---

## TET2
**In one line:** An enzyme that helps "reset" the chemical tags on your DNA that drift with age.

**TET2** helps manage the chemical tags sitting on your DNA that switch genes on and off — a pattern that drifts as you age (part of the "epigenetic clock"). The supplement Ca-AKG feeds enzymes like this, and a small human study suggested it lowered measures of biological age.

Think of it as helping tidy up the DNA's control settings, which get messy over the years. Promising, early.

---

## TF
**In one line:** The iron-carrier protein — iron is what lets your blood carry oxygen.

**TF** (transferrin) ferries **iron** through your blood to where it's needed — above all, to build haemoglobin, the molecule in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Low iron means gasping endurance and constant fatigue, long before it shows up as anaemia — common in menstruating women and endurance athletes.

Crucial safety note: never supplement iron without testing first, because too much iron is toxic and damages organs. Test, then treat.

---

## THRB
**In one line:** The thyroid-hormone receptor — your body's metabolic thermostat.

**THRB** is a receptor for **thyroid hormone**, which sets your whole-body metabolic rate — how fast you burn energy. Too little and everything slows (fatigue, weight gain, cold); the right amount keeps metabolism humming.

Thyroid medication works here to treat an underactive thyroid. Some physique competitors misuse it to burn faster, but that also burns muscle and suppresses your own thyroid — so it's a legitimate medicine and a risky shortcut, depending on how it's used.

---

## TLR9
**In one line:** An immune "alarm" sensor that thymosin alpha-1 uses to tune up your defences.

**TLR9** is a sensor your immune cells use to detect invaders and raise the alarm. The peptide thymosin alpha-1 works partly through such sensors to mature and strengthen your T-cells (immune soldiers). It's an approved immune therapy in many countries for chronic infections, though investigational in the US.

Think of it as sharpening the immune system's ability to spot and respond to threats.

---

## TMSB4X
**In one line:** The gene behind TB-500, a peptide that helps repair cells reach a wound.

**TMSB4X** makes **thymosin beta-4**, a protein that helps repair cells physically migrate to sites of damage, grow new blood vessels, and calm inflammation. The peptide TB-500 is a fragment of it, used (usually alongside BPC-157) to try to speed recovery.

Like most healing peptides, the animal data is interesting but human evidence is essentially anecdotal — a "know the risks and decide for yourself" compound.

---

## TPH2
**In one line:** The factory enzyme that builds serotonin, your calm-and-contentment chemical.

**TPH2** is the rate-limiting (slowest, controlling) enzyme that manufactures **serotonin** in the brain — the chemical behind calm, contentment, and healthy sleep. Supplements like 5-HTP skip past this bottleneck by supplying serotonin's near-finished ingredient directly.

Because they raise serotonin, they should never be combined with antidepressants — too much serotonin at once is dangerous.

---

## TRPM6
**In one line:** The doorway that carries magnesium into your body.

**TRPM6** is a transporter that absorbs **magnesium** — a mineral your body uses in over 300 reactions, including making energy, calming the nervous system, and quality sleep. Roughly half of people don't get enough, and athletes lose it in sweat.

Topping up magnesium (especially the glycinate form at night) reliably improves sleep and eases muscle cramps — this doorway is simply how it gets in.

---

## UCP1
**In one line:** A "heater" protein in fat that burns energy as pure heat.

**UCP1** sits in special "brown fat" and does something unusual: it lets your cellular power plants run without making usable energy, releasing the fuel as **heat** instead. This is how the body warms itself, and it's a way to burn calories.

Fat-burners like clenbuterol partly work by turning this heater up. The dangerous extreme is the banned chemical DNP, which forces this wasteful heat-burning everywhere at once — it melts fat but can literally cook you from the inside, and it kills people. A vivid lesson in how the same mechanism can be helpful or lethal by degree.

---

## MSTN
**In one line:** The body's "stop building muscle" brake — blocking it lets muscles grow bigger.

**MSTN** (myostatin) is a protein your body uses to *cap* how much muscle you can build — a built-in brake so muscles don't grow without limit. Animals (and rare humans) with a broken myostatin gene are extraordinarily muscular. Experimental "myostatin inhibitors" (and the compound follistatin, which neutralises it) aim to release this brake.

The promise is obvious; the reality is that human safety is unknown and early drugs in this class were stopped for side effects. A compelling idea that isn't proven or safe yet.
